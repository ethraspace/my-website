Extreme Environments
What hostile conditions teach civilian design.

Defense and space programmes hold decades of human factors research that remains largely outside civilian design practice. ETHRA works at this boundary.

ENV 01 · POLAR · minus 89°C · 9 months
Antarctica
  • Circadian lighting protocols from Concordia and Halley VI now inform ETHRA Studio's north-facing apartment work
  • Modular spatial reconfiguration tested at polar stations applied to flexible urban residential typologies
  • Biophilic material systems maintain psychological health in total darkness and low-light domestic environments
ENV 02 · DESERT · 55°C · zero humidity
Sahara and Arabian Peninsula
  • Phase-change materials from military forward operating bases deliver 60% cooling energy reductions
  • Passive thermal mass systems applied as invisible infrastructure, not technical aesthetic
  • Wind-driven ventilation geometries developed for survival now inform ETHRA Studio's luxury desert commissions
ENV 03 · ORBITAL · 408 km · microgravity
Low Earth Orbit
  • ISS water recycling efficiency of 93% has direct applications in water-stressed residential contexts
  • HEPA and photocatalytic filtration systems entering civilian construction via ETHRA Lab adaptation frameworks
  • Acoustic damping and spatial compression techniques developed for orbital modules applicable to high-density urban housing

Interior Architecture · Space Design
Interior Architecture Beyond Earth

The history of interior design has always been shaped by constraint. Stone, then timber, then steel defined what rooms could be. Now the constraint is atmosphere itself. Designing habitats for orbital environments forces a reckoning with every assumption the discipline has taken for granted. What emerges is not a futuristic aesthetic but a set of deeper principles that apply equally to a penthouse in London and a habitat in low Earth orbit.

From Personal Fascination to Design Research

Every practice begins with an obsession. For ETHRA, it began with a simple question asked at the wrong scale: what does it actually feel like to be inside a space designed for survival rather than comfort? The answer led from Antarctic expedition reports to NASA human factors research, and eventually to the conviction that luxury interior design and space habitat engineering are not opposites but the same inquiry at different pressures.
